Drainage, Arctic floodplain

(MPI-BGC; PAGE21, BMBF)

Background: Wet and moist ecosystems are common in the Arctic due to the existence of permafrost that impedes vertical soil water drainage. When permafrost thaws and ground ice melts, these ecosystems can become wetter or drier. The objective of this project is to quantify the changes in CO2 and CH4 fluxes following drainage on an Arctic floodplain in northeast Siberia, Chersky.

Findings: Drainage increased CO2 emission rates largely through warmer soil temperature at the top soil, which accelerated aerobic decomposition. Drainage, however, substantially reduced CH4 emission rates mainly due to changes from anaerobic to aerobic soils, and it was facilitated by reductions in CH4-associated microbial communities, soil temperatures (where CH4 is largely produced), and abundance of sedges (which transport CH4 from soils to the atmosphere). Another interesting fact is, due to the strong insulating effects of dry peat (organic soil), drainage of wet tundra can protect permafrost from thawing.

Participated in 2012-2016

Snow manipulation

(KOPRI; CAPEC)

Background: Snow plays a critical role in ecosystem functions. When it snows more, 1) thicker snowpack keeps soil temperature warmer through insulation, 2) more snow provides more water to soil when snow melts, 3) more snow shortens summer growing season because it takes longer for snow to melt, 4) more snow-covered days reduces incoming energy into terrestrial ecosystems by increasing days with high albedo, and etc. By building snow fences perpendicular to the prevailing wind direction in winter, we manipulated snow depth in dry tundra in northern Canada, Cambridge Bay and in moist tundra in Council, Alaska in 2017.

Findings: Our initial results showed, warmer winter soil temperature, but delayed snow melt and soil thaw, and enhanced vegetation greenness with increased snowfall. We will continue tracing how ecosystems evolve, by monitoring soil temperature and moisture, microbial functions, soil nutrients, and greenhouse gas emissions.

Participated in 2017-2019

Temperature and Rain manipulation

(KOPRI; CAPEC)

Background: Air temperature in the Arctic has risen twice faster than the global average increase rate, and is expected to further increase in the future. Precipitation patterns vary locally and temporally, and those in northern Canada are expected to increase in the future. Some researchers at KOPRI set up open top chambers (OTC) to increase air temperature and added water every week during summer season to mimic increased rainfall in dry tundra in Cambridge Bay, Canada since 2012. We observe how warming (increased air temperature) and/or wetting (increased rainfall) modify plant biomass and communities, microbial activities, soil carbon and nutrients, and CO2 fluxes.

Drainage, northern peatlands

(LSCE; MOPGA)

Background: Northern peatlands have accumulated >400 Pg of carbon due to warm and wet conditions. When permafrost thaw increases drainage, or warmer temperature enhances evapotranspiration, water table of northern peatlands decreases, exposing easily-decomposable peat to dry condition. Lowered water table decreases CH4 emissions but increases CO2 emissions by accelerating aerobic decomposition, potentially leading to a positive feedback to warming. We quantify how much CO2 and CH4 fluxes change due to drainage in northern peatlands, using a land surface model ORCHIDEE-PEAT-METHANE that is calibrated by flux observation from water table manipulation experiments.
